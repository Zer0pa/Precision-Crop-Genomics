# Precision crop genomics — full technology landscape (Report 1 of 2)

**Zer0pa can ship a commercially deployable, zero-IP-liability precision crop genomics pipeline today using a roughly 25-tool open stack — but only if it routes around four well-defined license traps and accepts that the pipeline's center of gravity is haplotype-aware genomic prediction with environment conditioning, not foundation-model G2P.** The architecture-critical breakthroughs of the last 24 months are pangenome graph genotyping (PHGv2 + Giraffe + PanGenie), plant DNA foundation models with permissive weights (PlantCaduceus, HyenaDNA, Evo-2, GPN, PlantRNA-FM), and the EU NGT regulation (adopted April 2026, applying mid-2028) — these reshape both the model layer and the regulatory router. The deployable surface for actual gene editing remains narrow (Arabidopsis, tomato, potato, rice, wheat-with-GRF-GIF, lettuce, *Nicotiana*, Camelina); maize-elite and sorghum scale only through Corteva's WUS2/BBM patent estate. South Africa is the binding regional constraint: Minister Didiza's January 2024 final decision keeps gene-edited crops under the full GMO Act, so Zer0pa's SA strategy must default to MAS-and-prediction pathways, with editing performed in Kenya/Nigeria/Mauritius and germplasm imported through conventional crossing. The real Zer0pa moat is not modeling — it is the **typed, license-aware, regulatory-routed handoff dossier**: a partner-ready package that no incumbent (Inari closed; Pairwise wet-lab licensable; Living Models foundation-only; Computomics service-based) currently delivers.

This report compresses a maximal sweep of the field into a v1 build map, layer by layer, with the license decomposition, benchmark grounding, and intersectional-science framing the pipeline architecture demands.

## Section 1. The v1 architecture, in one diagram and four sentences

The pipeline takes five typed inputs — **crop species, agroecological zone, target trait set, breeding-or-editing constraints, regulatory route constraints** — plus optional germplasm, pangenome, and phenotype context. It produces a single typed output: a **Crop Improvement Dossier** containing ranked candidate interventions, trait-performance forecasts, environment-conditioned predictions, an execution specification, and a partner handoff package.

The internal flow is six layers: **(L1) ZPE-encoded multimodal inputs → (L2) license-tagged knowledge layer → (L3) candidate generation across cross/MAS/edit modalities → (L4) in silico screening with G×E and regulatory scoring → (L5) Bayesian active-learning optimization → (L6) typed dossier output**. The orchestration backbone is LangGraph; the optimizer is BoTorch (qNEHVI for multi-objective Pareto fronts over yield × resilience × nutrition × input efficiency × regulatory friction); the canonical genotype store is PLINK 2 PGEN with PHGv2 (TileDB-VCF backend) for haplotypes; the canonical breeding-data spine is BrAPI v2.1 over self-hosted Breedbase. Every record carries a four-column license stamp (code / data / weights / API), and every model artifact is filterable by license class so derived predictions are demonstrably commercializable.

**The single most important architectural choice** is making pangenome-aware modeling first-class for the seven Tier-1 species (maize, rice, wheat, barley, sorghum, soybean, tomato) where graph genotyping captures 10–30% of trait variance invisible to SNP-only references — and gracefully degrading to SNP-only mode for orphan crops where pangenomes do not yet exist.

## Section 2. Layer 1 — input encoding and the ZPE schema

The input encoding layer accepts six modalities with explicit type contracts. **Genotype** comes in as VCF/BCF, PLINK BED/BIM/FAM, PLINK2 PGEN/PVAR/PSAM (canonical internal), HapMap, or PHGv2 hVCF; SNPs, dosage, phased haplotypes, and structural variants from graph genotyping all flow through PGEN as the canonical store. **Pangenome paths** use GFA 1.1/2 and rGFA, with PanSN naming (`sample#hap#contig`) as the universal identifier. **Phenotype + trial metadata** uses MIAPPE 1.1 over BrAPI v2.1, with ISA-Tab and RO-Crate as serialization options. **Environment** uses xarray/COG rasters joined to trial GPS via Zer0pa-built ETL (no upstream tool exists). **Trait ontologies** are Crop Ontology (per-crop trait dictionaries, all CC-BY 4.0), Plant Ontology, Plant Trait Ontology, Plant Stress Ontology, ENVO, and PECO. **Time-series phenomics** comes in as PlantCV-compatible image stacks with logistic-curve fits over GDD.

The ZPE encoding question — can a single learned encoder handle genomic tokens, allele stacks, pangenome paths, marker blocks, and environment-conditioned trait descriptors — has a real answer in 2026. **PlantCaduceus (Apache-2.0 weights)** handles single-nucleotide DNA at the cross-species level using BiMamba with reverse-complement equivariance, and is the only plant DNA foundation model that is both architecturally novel (RC-equivariant) and commercially licensable. **HyenaDNA-large-1m (BSD-3 weights)** handles 1 Mb context for long-range regulatory neighborhoods. **Evo-2 (Apache-2.0)** spans all domains of life including some plants. For environment encoding, **EnvRtype (MIT)** + **AgERA5 (CC-BY 4.0)** + **CHELSA (CC-BY)** + **CHIRPS v3 (CC0)** produce stable embeddings; WorldClim 2.1 must be excluded — it is CC-BY-NC-SA despite ubiquitous academic use, and its appearance in any commercial Zer0pa pipeline would taint outputs.

## Section 3. Layer 2 — knowledge layer, with the license trap inventory

The production knowledge stack reduces to twelve must-wire resources with clean four-column licensing. The genome-and-protein backbone is **Ensembl Plants** (Apache-2.0 software, effectively CC-BY data, REST + bulk + BioMart), **NCBI RefSeq** (US public domain), **UniProt** (CC-BY 4.0 since 2022), **InterPro** (CC-BY 4.0), and **Gramene** with Plant Reactome (CC-BY-style). The semantic layer is the OBO Foundry suite — **Crop Ontology, Plant Ontology, Plant Trait Ontology, Plant Stress Ontology, PECO, ENVO** — all CC-BY 4.0 or CC0. The breeding spine is **self-hosted Breedbase (MIT)** speaking BrAPI 2.1, complemented by **MaizeGDB / SoyBase / LIS / Cassavabase / SweetpotatoBase / MusaBase / YamBase / SGN** (all USDA-ARS US-PD or MIT/CC-BY). The germplasm layer is **GRIN-Global (US-PD)** as the cleanest legal substrate, with **Genesys** for global query (custom Crop Trust terms permit query but not competing-aggregator redistribution) and direct CGIAR genebank APIs (IRRI, CIMMYT, ICRISAT, ICARDA, IITA, AfricaRice, all CGIAR Open Access). The G×E forcing layer is **AgERA5 + ERA5-Land (CC-BY)**, **SoilGrids 2.0 (CC-BY)**, **CHIRPS v3 (CC0)**, and **TerraClimate (CC0)**.

Five license traps must be coded as hard exclusions in the data ingestion tier. **PLAZA** (Ghent comparative genomics) is CC-BY-NC-SA — explicitly excludes industrial-sponsored academic work. **TAIR live curated data** requires a paid Phoenix Bioinformatics commercial subscription (~$28k/yr large company); only the public release more than one year old is CC-BY 4.0, and the safe path is to pull TAIR12 directly from GenBank. **WorldClim 2.1** is non-commercial despite ubiquitous use; **CHELSA** and **AgERA5** are drop-in replacements. **EOX Sentinel-2 cloudless mosaics** are CC-BY-NC-SA, while raw Sentinel-2 from ESA is fully open commercial. **JGI/Phytozome embargoed data** carries a 2-year redistribution restriction. Beyond the hard exclusions, three softer traps require row-level handling: **T3 (Triticeae Toolbox)** carries Toronto Statement pre-publication restrictions on some wheat/barley/oat data, **CGIAR genebank physical seed** moves under SMTA (ITPGRFA Annex 1), and the **Doench/Fusi sgRNA scoring weights** bundled into many CRISPR design tools have Microsoft Research redistribution constraints.

The Zer0pa-built piece in the knowledge layer is the **license-stamping ETL service**: every record ingested gets a four-column license tag, a Toronto-embargo expiry timestamp where applicable, an SMTA flag for physical material, and a downstream filter that prevents any model training run from mixing class-A and class-C/D inputs into a commercializable artifact. No upstream tool does this — it is a Zer0pa-owned data primitive.

## Section 4. Layer 3 — candidate generation across breeding and editing modalities

Candidate generation splits into three independent sub-pipelines that converge at the screening layer.

**Cross-and-MAS design** runs on **AlphaSimR (MIT)** as the de facto standard breeding simulator, with **MoBPS (GPL-3)** as the alternative when bitwise SNP storage and cohort-economic modeling are needed and **ChromaX (BSD-3, JAX/GPU)** as the high-throughput backend when ML coupling demands two-orders-of-magnitude speedup. Cross-design uses **AlphaMate (academic free, Fortran)** or **PyBrOpS (Apache-2.0)** for multi-objective Pareto allocation, **optiSel (GPL-2)** for optimal contribution selection with diversity constraints, and **selectiongain (GPL-2/3)** for analytical multi-stage budget optimization. **MateSel** is the commercial gold standard but proprietary and per-species licensed; the AlphaMate + PyBrOpS open path covers Zer0pa needs without royalty. Field-trial design uses **FielDHub (MIT)** for Shiny-driven row-column / p-rep / sparse-testing layouts, **DiGGer** (free academic) where extra design optimality is needed, and **statgenSTA + statgenGxE (GPL)** with **SpATS (GPL)** for spatial trend correction — all of which combined cleanly replace the proprietary ASReml-R + CycDesigN stack at zero license cost, although factor-analytic FA(k) MET models still require ASReml or a careful sommer port.

**Edit design** splits sharply along IP exposure. The cleanest open commercial-friendly stack is **CROPSR (Apache-2.0)** for guide design in complex/polyploid crop genomes, **CRISPR-PLANT v2** (open, plant-tuned for gap/bulge off-targets), **Cas-OFFinder (BSD)** plus **CRISPRitz** for off-target enumeration, and **Variant-aware Cas-OFFinder** for SNP-aware predictions in 557 species. **CRISPOR** is excellent but its post-v4 licensing requires UCSC fees for non-academic use; treat as research-only. **Benchling guide design** is closed SaaS with vendor data exposure. **Synthego ICE** is non-commercial. **CRISPResso2** (open) handles edit verification. The chemistry layer requires explicit IP-routing: **SpCas9** needs a Broad–Pioneer ag pool license plus likely a CVC/Caribou sublicense for commercial crop products; **Cas12a** has cleaner Broad non-exclusive ag licensing; **base editors (ABE8e, CBE)** route through Pairwise's Liu/Broad sublicense; **prime editors (PE2/PE3)** route through Pairwise narrow ag rights, though **ePPE/PE6c** from CAS-Beijing offer accessible Chinese academic IP with 3.5–33× efficiency improvements over PE3 in cereals. **Base editing is materially more deployable than prime editing** in 2026: ABE8e routinely delivers 30–80% A→G in rice/wheat versus 0–25% with extreme variance for prime editing. v1 should default to base editing, reserve prime editing for R&D.

**Transformation and regeneration** is the actual bottleneck. The realistic Zer0pa v1 deployment set covers Tier-1 species (Arabidopsis, *Nicotiana*, tomato, rice japonica, potato, lettuce, Camelina) using standard Agrobacterium and ribonucleoprotein delivery on open-IP methods, plus Tier-2 species (wheat, barley, citrus, triticale) by securing **UC Davis GRF-GIF non-exclusive licensing** — the Debernardi 2020 chimera lifts wheat transformation efficiency from ~5% (Fielder) to 77.5% with cytokinin-independent regeneration. Tier-3 species (maize elites, sorghum, sugarcane) are blocked by **Corteva's WUS2/BBM patent estate** (US7256322 et seq.), which is the most defensible regeneration IP in the world; Zer0pa's leverage paths around this are GRF-GIF substitution where it works, HI-Edit / haploid-inducer-mediated editing (also IP-encumbered by Syngenta/Corteva but with academic Chinese alternatives), or **VIGE — virus-induced genome editing** using BSMV in wheat/barley, FoMV in maize, TRV/PVX in tomato/*Nicotiana*. VIGE is the single best regulatory and IP arbitrage move available: viral vectors come from dispersed academic IP, the final plant carries no integrated foreign DNA, and most permissive jurisdictions treat VIGE-edited products as transgene-free SDN-1.

## Section 5. Layer 4 — screening with genomic prediction, G×E, and regulatory routing

Screening combines four parallel scoring tracks whose outputs concatenate into a multi-objective vector for the optimizer.

**Genomic prediction** uses a tiered approach grounded in clear empirical evidence. The 2025 Montesinos-López et al. multi-dataset comparison and the Crossa 2024 *Trends in Plant Science* review converge on the same finding: **for highly polygenic, additive, large-N field traits, GBLUP remains state-of-the-art or tied with deep learning**; deep learning's wins are concentrated in non-additive traits (hybrid heterosis), small-N regimes, and multimodal integration. Typical predictive ability ranges are 0.30–0.50 for wheat yield within environment, 0.40–0.65 for maize within environment, 0.40–0.65 for rice within sub-population. The clean v1 prediction stack is **rrBLUP (GPL-3)** for the GBLUP baseline, **BGLR (GPL-3)** for the full Bayesian alphabet (BayesA/B/C/L/RKHS) and Jarquín reaction-norm models, and **sommer (GPL-3)** for multi-trait GBLUP with dominance, epistasis, and 2D spatial splines — together replacing the proprietary ASReml-R workflow. **DNNGP / DeepGP** can be added as optional deep tracks for small-N or non-additive traits; both currently lack formal LICENSE files in their GitHub repos and should be re-implemented under Zer0pa's own MIT license.

**G×E prediction** is the highest-leverage axis. Best practice in 2026 is **Jarquín reaction-norm GBLUP** (`G + E + GxE` kernel sum) using **EnvRtype (MIT)** for env data ingestion plus **BGGE (GPL-3)** for Bayesian inference; environmental kernels should use Gaussian similarity over standardized weather/soil features within critical growth windows identified by **CERIS-JGRA**. For drought- or heat-tolerance traits where statistical models stall, **CGM-GS** (crop growth model + genomic prediction) using **DSSAT-CSM (BSD-3)** as the open mechanistic substrate yields 10–30% relative gains. **APSIM must not be used commercially** without paid licensing — its Special Non-Commercial License is the single biggest license trap in the crop modeling stack and Zer0pa should treat APSIM as if it were proprietary even though its source is publicly viewable. **AquaCrop-OSPy (GPL-3)** is the second-clean alternative for water-stress modeling, and **PCSE/WOFOST (EUPL)** rounds out the open CGM toolkit.

**Pangenome-aware screening** is now architecture-critical for Tier-1 cereals. Tomato graph pangenome work (Zhou *Nature* 2022) showed +24% heritability capture from SV-aware GWAS; rice super-pangenome work (Qin 2022) and wheat pangenome work (Jiao *Nature* 2024) confirm equivalent gains. The deployable open stack is **PHGv2 (Apache-2.0)** with TileDB-VCF backend for haplotype indexing and skim-sequencing imputation (~$3–7/sample), **vg/Giraffe (MIT)** for graph short-read alignment (~$0.50–2/sample), **PanGenie (MIT)** for k-mer-based SV genotyping, **Sniffles2 (MIT)** for de novo long-read SV calling, and **minigraph-cactus (MIT)** as the preferred graph builder for breeding (linear-coordinate-preserving) over PGGB (better for evolutionary super-pangenomes including wild relatives). For polyploids, **Varigraph** is the emerging option but less mature than diploid PanGenie. Zer0pa's positioning here is consumer-and-integrator, not builder — public pangenomes for the 12–15 most economically relevant crops already exist under CC-BY, and de novo construction costs more than $0.5M/species in long-read sequencing.

**Off-target and regulatory route fit** are the under-built screening axes where Zer0pa can differentiate. The off-target layer enumerates ≤4 mismatches plus 1 bulge across the full polyploid genome with homoeologs treated as a target group rather than off-targets, scores base-editor windows on every off-target, and flags sgRNA-independent enzyme deamination risk. The regulatory route fit layer — described in Section 9 — converts edit type, trait class, target jurisdictions, and delivery method into a composite Regulatory Fit Score that feeds the Pareto optimizer alongside genetic gain.

## Section 6. Layer 5 — Bayesian optimization, active learning, and the open frontier

Layer 5 is the layer where the literature and the production tooling diverge most sharply. **No production-grade BoTorch-for-breeding tool exists** as of May 2026, despite three lines of solid academic work: scheme-level Bayesian optimization (Diot & Iwata 2023; Azam et al. 2024), training-set BO for genomic prediction (J. Agric. Biol. Environ. Stat. 2024), and reinforcement learning over real maize data (Younis et al. 2024 with released Gym environments beating standard practice). Sparse-testing optimization (Werner & Tolhurst 2025; Lubanga et al. 2025) is the operational complement: partially-replicated farm-as-incomplete-block designs deliver substantially higher genetic gain than fully-replicated CRBs. The clean architecture is to wrap **AlphaSimR or ChromaX as a black-box simulator** behind a **BoTorch GP surrogate** with qNEHVI multi-objective acquisition; this is the highest-impact piece Zer0pa can build itself, and it is also the closest analog to the Bayesian active-learning loops in the broader Zer0pa platform.

The optimization output should always be a **Pareto front over (yield gain, drought resilience, nutrient efficiency, nutritional quality, regulatory friction, deployment ROI)** rather than a scalar selection index. Multi-objective is not optional for Zer0pa: the value proposition over Inari/Pairwise's closed-loop optimizers is exactly that breeders and seed companies retain agency over tradeoff selection on a curated front, rather than receiving a single black-box recommendation.

Two adjacent gaps that change architecture rather than merely improve scoring are **causal inference for trait discovery** and **active inference for trial prioritization**. Causal inference is essentially absent from production breeding — the field remains mixed-model regression on `phenotype = µ + G + E + GxE + spatial + ε` — but tools exist (DoWhy, causal-learn, cGAUGE, MendelianRandomization R package; CI-GWAS demonstrated multi-trait causal graphs from 17 traits in under 10 minutes on GPU). Treating markers as instrumental variables and structuring G2P discovery as a PC-algorithm graph plus Mendelian-randomization effect-size pruning reframes the entire screening loop. Active inference (Sajid et al.; pymdp) makes the whole pipeline a sequential belief-update POMDP where each phenotyping decision, each genotyping decision, and each cross is chosen by expected information gain about treatment-effect heterogeneity rather than by predictive R². Both are frontier moves Zer0pa could ship as v1.5 differentiation.

## Section 7. Layer 6 — the typed handoff dossier

The handoff schema must be designed before the pipeline is built, because every other layer's output type contract depends on it. The Crop Improvement Dossier is a versioned, signed, machine-readable JSON-LD document with the following typed sections, each backed by JSON Schema with explicit license-class fields.

The **Identity block** carries the dossier UUID, version, target crop (NCBI taxonomy ID + Crop Ontology vocabulary ID), target agroecological zone (CIMMYT TPE classification + GPS bounding polygon + CMIP6 scenario tag for forward projections), target trait set (Crop Ontology TO IDs with measurement methods and units), and the set of inputs hashed for reproducibility. The **Intervention Strategy block** carries a ranked list of candidates — each tagged as `cross_design`, `mas_marker_set`, `crispr_edit`, `base_edit`, `prime_edit`, `vige_edit`, or `cisgenic_insertion` — with full execution specifications: parental genotypes for crosses, marker IDs for MAS, sgRNA sequences plus PAMs plus delivery method for edits, donor templates where applicable, and explicit transformation/regeneration protocols including the IP-licensing requirement (Corteva WUS2/BBM, UC Davis GRF-GIF, etc.). The **Performance Forecast block** carries genomic estimated breeding values per trait with 95% credible intervals, environment-specific predictions across the target zone's representative trial sites and forward-projected climate scenarios, factor-analytic G×E variance components, and a calibrated confidence score derived from the kernel of the prediction model. The **Risk Block** carries off-target risk profiles for edits (including homoeolog-aware polyploid analysis and base-editor enzyme deamination risk), transformation feasibility scores per cultivar, regeneration-time estimates with morphogenic-regulator IP flags, and pleiotropy warnings from gene-network centrality analysis. The **Regulatory Block** carries the per-jurisdiction route classification (full GMO / NGT-1 / NGT-2 / non-GMO / notification-only) with the citation chain to current regulatory text, the composite Regulatory Fit Score per target market, the list of triggering trait modifiers (HT, PIP, allergen, pharma), and the Cartagena/Nagoya/SMTA documentation chain. The **Provenance Block** carries the four-column license stamp for every input dataset, model artifact, and tool used — with an attestation that no class-C/D inputs contaminated commercializable predictions — plus the Zer0pa pipeline version, model checksums, and BrAPI-compliant export endpoints. The **Partner Handoff Block** is the last section and varies by recipient: a seed company gets cultivar-ready breeding execution timelines and SACTA-compatible cultivar registration documentation for Southern Africa; a public breeding institute gets germplasm-tracking IDs and ITPGRFA-compatible benefit-sharing notes; a CRISPR editing partner gets full molecular cloning specifications; a regional agricultural program gets adoption-modeling and farmer-deployment fit scores integrated with HarvestPlus Biofortification Priority Index where relevant.

The dossier must be **regenerable from inputs at any time** — no implicit state, no hidden context, no untracked manual decisions — and must emit attributions for every CC-BY data source automatically. This is not a documentation feature; it is the legal substrate that makes a Zer0pa-produced dossier safer for partners than an Inari-produced one.

## Section 8. Phenomics, remote sensing, and the cases where they actually move prediction

The phenomics and remote-sensing layer earns its place in the architecture only where it materially improves breeding prediction. The clean v1 stack is **PlantCV (MPL-2.0)** plus **pliman (GPL-3)** plus **Detectron2 / SAM2 / DINOv2 (Apache-2.0 weights)** plus **MicMac (CECILL-B)** for orthomosaics — explicitly avoiding **OpenDroneMap** (AGPL-3.0 with network-use trigger that forces source disclosure for any hosted Zer0pa SaaS) and **Ultralytics YOLOv5/v8** (AGPL-3.0 with required Enterprise license; substitute YOLOX or MMDetection). The minimum useful sensor stack for materially improving genomic prediction is UAV multispectral (RedEdge-P or Mavic 3M), UAV thermal (FLIR Vue or Workswell WIRIS), UAV RGB sub-cm GSD, RTK GNSS for plot georeferencing, and Sentinel-1 + Sentinel-2 + HLS time series for environmental covariates. The empirical literature is clear on where the lift comes from: Krause et al. 2019 (G3) showed hyperspectral-derived relationship matrices add ~10% predictive ability to GBLUP for wheat yield, and more under stress; Anderegg et al. 2024 showed even consumer-drone multispectral helps; Robert et al. 2022 showed grain NIR spectra produce a "phenomic H-matrix" that augments G-BLUP particularly when genotyping is sparse — a cheap, robust path for low-resource breeding programs. Single-timepoint Sentinel-2 NDVI on standard 1.5m × 6m breeding plots is theatrical and should be excluded from v1.

The compute-platform decision is non-trivial and license-loaded. **Google Earth Engine is not commercially usable on the free tier** for any private company, including pre-revenue Zer0pa work; the paid commercial subscription is a per-EECU-hour billing on top of a tier subscription. The license-clean alternative is **AWS Open Data Registry plus Microsoft Planetary Computer Pro plus xarray/rioxarray/STAC plus DASK**, which gives full Sentinel-2 / Landsat / HLS / Sentinel-1 / MODIS access under genuinely open licenses with predictable Azure/AWS billing. For South African operations specifically, AWS `af-south-1` (Cape Town) keeps data residency local and preserves compatibility with smallholder field sizes that frequently fall below Sentinel-2's 10m mixed-pixel resolution; PlanetScope (commercial, 3m daily) becomes a paid layer for sub-600m² fields where Jain 2021 demonstrated material classification gains.

There is currently **no production "PlantCLIP" or unified plant-image foundation model**. The community uses general-purpose vision foundation models — DINOv2, SAM2, MAE — adapted via LoRA for plant tasks (Chen, Giuffrida, Tsaftaris ICCVW 2023). NASA-IBM's **Prithvi-EO-2.0 (Apache-2.0)** and **Clay v1 (Apache-2.0)** are the closest things to a "satellite-CLIP" with permissive weights. The TasselNetV4 work (2025) markets itself as a foundation model for plant counting. Building a Zer0pa-owned plant image foundation model on TERRA-REF (CC0) plus GWHD (MIT) plus PhenoBench (CC-BY-SA 4.0) plus aggregated GBIF Pl@ntNet imagery is a clear frontier opportunity worth analyzing in Report 2.

## Section 9. Regulatory routing as a first-class scoring axis

The regulatory layer is not a downstream filter; it is a scoring axis with the same weight as predicted yield. Three hard facts shape the routing logic.

**The EU NGT regulation was adopted by Council on 21 April 2026** with Parliament plenary scheduled for 18 May 2026, OJEU publication during 2026, and application from approximately mid-2028 after a 24-month transition. Until application, the 2018 Confédération Paysanne ECJ ruling stands and all gene-edited plants are full GMOs. Post-application, NGT-1 (≤20-nt modifications, equivalent to conventional, **excluded for herbicide tolerance and known insecticidal substances**) is treated as conventional with seed-only labeling but banned from organic; NGT-2 (everything else) keeps the full GMO regime with member-state opt-outs. **Designing for NGT-1 starts now**, not in 2028 — every Zer0pa-recommended edit should be ≤20nt and avoid HT/PIP traits unless EU and SA markets are explicitly out of scope.

**South Africa's January 2024 Section-19 ministerial decision keeps gene-edited crops under the GMO Act 1997**, putting SA in conflict with Kenya, Nigeria, Ghana, Malawi, Rwanda — the rest of the operationally important African biotech jurisdictions, all of which exempt SDN-1/SDN-2 with no foreign DNA from their biosafety regimes. The structural workaround is to perform CRISPR work in Kenya/Nigeria/Mauritius, complete non-GMO determination in the origin jurisdiction, and import the resulting trait into SA via conventional crossing under SACTA-recognized cultivar registration — accepting that SA cannot be a near-term ag deployment market for edited products absent GMO Act amendment.

**The U.S. SECURE Rule was vacated on 2 December 2024** (National Family Farm Coalition v. Vilsack); APHIS published technical conforming amendments effective 16 June 2025 reverting to the pre-2020 regime; a replacement rule (RIN 0579-AE84) is expected in 2026. The pre-SECURE regime is actually more permissive for SDN-1 with no plant pest, but anything using Agrobacterium triggers permits.

The Regulatory Fit Score Zer0pa applies as a Pareto axis is a market-weighted product:

> RFS = Σ over target markets [ MarketWeight × JurisdictionScore(0–5) × TraitMultiplier(0.3–1.0) × EditTypeCoefficient(0.2–1.0) ] − ProcessRiskPenalty

Jurisdiction scores follow the master decision matrix: 5 for Israel and US-SDN-1-no-plant-pest; 4 for Argentina, Brazil, Canada, Japan, India, Australia (SDN-1 only), England (PBO regime since November 2025); 3 for China gene-edit fast track and post-2028 EU NGT-1 and Kenya/Nigeria/Philippines/Thailand; 2 for Uganda/Zimbabwe/Mexico/post-Bill New Zealand; 1 for pre-2028 EU, South Africa, current New Zealand, South Korea; 0 for prohibited categories (gene drives, cultivation bans). Trait multipliers heavily penalize herbicide tolerance (0.50 — automatic NGT-2 exclusion in EU), allergen modification (0.40), and pharma traits (0.30); they reward yield, disease resistance, abiotic-stress, and nutritional traits (1.0). Edit type coefficients reward SDN-1 RNP delivery (1.0), VIGE delivery (0.80), and base editing (0.95) while penalizing cisgenesis >20nt (0.55) and full transgenesis (0.20). The composite RFS feeds qNEHVI as one of six objectives; thresholds at 4.0 (green light), 2.5 (yellow with mitigation), and below 2.5 (red) drive routing decisions.

The single trait class that triggers special treatment beyond the GMO/non-GMO split, and which Zer0pa should flag prominently in every dossier, is **herbicide tolerance** — automatic NGT-2 in the EU regardless of edit size, separate EPA registration in the US, ANVISA pesticide review in Brazil, full GMO in SA. Insect resistance via novel insecticidal protein triggers EPA PIP registration and EU NGT-1 exclusion; **loss-of-function susceptibility-gene knockouts** are the architecturally cleaner path to insect resistance across all jurisdictions.

## Section 10. The intersectional science map — where it changes architecture and where it changes scoring

Eight cross-domain disciplines materially change Zer0pa's pipeline architecture rather than merely tuning scoring features.

**Information theory** introduces a Tishby-style bottleneck encoder layer that compresses high-dimensional marker inputs into trait-relevant latents using conditional mutual information rather than predictive loss; this is a different optimizer, not a regularization tweak. **Graph theory** turns the substrate from feature-vector ML into GNN message-passing over heterogeneous knowledge graphs (gene–variant–trait–environment–cultivar), with eigenvector and betweenness centrality as priors on marker effect. **Control theory** adds an ODE/PDE dynamic-systems layer for time-varying traits — flowering circuits, drought response, senescence — and enables MPC-style multi-cycle breeding-scheme planning rather than single-cycle optimization. **SE(3)-equivariant geometry** introduces a 3D structure tower for any candidate NLR receptor, photosynthetic enzyme, transporter, or transcription factor DBD, using e3nn / EquiformerV2 / RFdiffusion3 over AF2 plant proteome predictions (CC-BY 4.0 since January 2022) — making protein-structure-aware trait engineering possible for the first time. **Causal inference** replaces correlational GWAS with do-calculus and Mendelian randomization, using markers as instrumental variables and structuring trial design as interventional rather than observational. **Climate physics** adds a learned environment encoder branch (daily Tmin/Tmax/RH/rain/VPD/soil → embedding) that meets the genome encoder in a joint latent space — turning GBLUP into a two-tower G×E-VAE and enabling forward projection onto CMIP6-2050 scenarios for climate-resilient cultivar selection. **Active inference** turns the breeding pipeline into a POMDP where every phenotyping, genotyping, and cross decision is chosen by expected information gain; this collapses the entire L4–L5 boundary into a single epistemic loop. **Systems ecology** treats the unit of selection as the holobiont rather than the genotype, adding microbiome PCs and rhizosphere functional-pathway abundance as covariates and enabling cultivars-that-recruit-beneficial-AMF as a heritable trait — particularly important for low-input African agriculture.

Six disciplines change scoring features without restructuring the architecture. **Statistical mechanics** contributes pairwise-Potts (DCA-style) Hamiltonian regularizers for traits with known threshold biology. **Evolutionary biology** contributes long-horizon NK-landscape gain estimates rather than single-cycle EBVs. **Signal processing** contributes wavelet-decomposed growth-curve coefficients and dynamic-time-warping kernels. **Computer vision** contributes plot-level secondary traits. **Optimization theory** contributes the Pareto-front-rather-than-scalar output. **Economics** contributes adoption-likelihood-weighted ROI scoring with HarvestPlus Biofortification Priority Index integration for biofortification programs and SACTA levy-share modeling for SA self-pollinated crops.

The architecture-changing list is also the prioritized v1.5/v2 development roadmap: each item collapses a layer boundary or adds a substrate Zer0pa would have to build itself, because no upstream tool currently delivers it.

## Section 11. Commercial landscape and Zer0pa's defensible position

The competitive set splits cleanly into vertically-integrated trait companies, foundation-model providers, service-based bioinformatics shops, and regulated wet-lab editing platforms. **Inari** ($771M raised, $2.17B valuation Jan 2025) is the closest analog in scope but operates as a closed germplasm-and-data flywheel — no external API, vertical integration from breeding through transformation. **Pairwise** ($155M raised) is the most aggressive licensor: their Fulcrum platform is sub-licensed to Bayer, Corteva (5-year JV via Catalyst), Tropic, CIMMYT, Enza Zaden, Genus, and academic partners — making Pairwise the most likely strategic partner for Zer0pa rather than competitor. **Tropic Biosciences** (~$214M raised, Series C March 2026) leads tropical crop editing (TR4 banana, coffee) with commercial deployment underway. **Phytoform Labs** (Corteva-partnered, June 2025) is the closest direct competitor on AI-driven cis-regulatory design, running their CRE.AI.TIVE pipeline on AWS HealthOmics. **Cibus** is a pure trait-licensing model (1,000 patents post-Calyxt merger) selling to seed companies. **Computomics** is service-based bioinformatics — ~$10–40M ARR-equivalent, project-based — and is a clear Zer0pa partner rather than competitor. **Living Models** released BOTANIC (open weights on Hugging Face) in March 2026 with $7M seed funding, aiming to be the "Hugging Face of plant genomics"; this is the closest direct technical peer and warrants close watching, possibly partnership rather than competition. **Benson Hill** filed Chapter 11 in March 2025 and Chapter 7 in September 2025 — its CropOS assets were acquired by Confluence Genetics; the lesson is that vertically-integrated grain supply chains paired with computational platforms are capital-fragile.

Among large seed companies, **Syngenta's Cropwise Open Platform** (opened to third-party developers November 2025, ~70M ha, 30+ countries) is the most actively partner-seeking corporate API and the obvious distribution channel for a Zer0pa platform play. **Corteva's Genlytix ecosystem** integrates Pairwise, Phytoform, Profluent, and Resurrect Bio through Catalyst investment; Corteva is the most active acquirer in the space and a likely long-run exit partner. **Bayer's Climate FieldView** is agronomic, not genomic, and not the right integration point. **KWS, Limagrain, BASF, Sakata, Takii, Rijk Zwaan, Enza Zaden** all run closed internal stacks.

Among data and infrastructure providers, **NRGene's GenoMAGIC and PanMAGIC** (used by Syngenta and Bayer/Monsanto) are pangenome-as-a-service offerings Zer0pa should consume rather than rebuild. **Hiphen Cloverfield** is a phenotyping API to integrate. **AWS HealthOmics** (with Phytoform's October 2025 case study showing 100× speedup with NVIDIA L4/L40S GPUs and Nextflow/WDL/CWL support) is the most likely cloud foundation. **Hugging Face** hosts AgroNT, BOTANIC, PlantCaduceus, GPN, PlantRNA-FM weights — Zer0pa should consume and fine-tune rather than pre-train from scratch. **BrAPI v2** is the open standard Zer0pa must speak both as consumer and provider.

Zer0pa's defensible position is structurally underfilled: a **BrAPI-native, open-weights-friendly, partner-friendly genomic prediction plus variant prioritization plus regulatory routing API** for mid-tier seed companies, CGIAR and NARES public breeders, and specialty-crop programs — too small for Inari/Pairwise enterprise pricing, too sophisticated for legacy CGIAR BMS/EBS tools. The zero-IP-liability framing is genuinely valuable; staying platform-only and refusing to take trait IP is the differentiator that lets Zer0pa partner with all five major seed companies simultaneously rather than picking a side.

## Section 12. South Africa, Southern Africa, and the orphan crop layer

Southern Africa's crop priorities, breeder ecosystem, and regulatory environment are different enough from the EU/US baseline that Zer0pa's regional layer must be architectural, not cosmetic. The crops that matter are white and yellow maize (3M ha SA, dominant SADC staple), wheat (Western Cape rain-fed plus Free State irrigated; SA imports ~50%), sugarcane, sunflower, soybean, sorghum, pearl millet (Namibia mahangu, Mozambique), cassava (Mozambique, eSwatini, Zambia), sweet potato, cowpea, plus the AOCC orphan crop priority list — bambara groundnut, marama bean, tef, finger millet, fonio. Disease pressures include MLN (East Africa creeping south), MSV, fall armyworm, **wheat stem rust Ug99** (5 lineage races confirmed in SA by ARC-Small Grain), sorghum anthracnose, banana TR4 (biosecurity priority, not yet detected in SA), and CBSD/CMD spreading southward in cassava.

The breeder ecosystem is anchored by ARC (with ARC-Grain Crops at Potchefstroom, ARC-Small Grain at Bethlehem, ARC-Tropical & Subtropical Crops at Nelspruit, ARC-Infruitec at Stellenbosch), CSIR Biosciences, ACGT, the ARC-Pretoria-CSIR axis, plus universities (Stellenbosch, UFS, UP, UKZN, NWU). Pannar (Corteva-owned since 2013) holds the strongest local maize germplasm; SeedCo (Zambia/regional) dominates African open hybrid maize; Klein Karoo, Capstone, Hygrotech, Sensako, and Limagrain-Zaad-SA cover diversified, vegetable, and cereal niches. **SACTA's breeding-and-technology levy on self-pollinated crops** (wheat, barley, oats, soybean, lupin) is the legal mechanism that compensates breeders by market share — without SACTA recognition there is no commercial ROI on SA-bred self-pollinated cultivars, and 20% of distributable funds are statutorily earmarked for transformation-empowered breeders. CIMMYT-Zimbabwe operates the Africa maize TPE; IITA hubs in Mozambique/Malawi/Zambia cover cassava/banana/cowpea; ICRISAT-Bulawayo covers sorghum/millet/legumes; AATF brokers PPP trait deployment (TELA Maize: 125+ conventional DroughtTEGO hybrids and 5 transgenic TELA hybrids deployed across Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania, Mozambique, Ethiopia, Nigeria, and SA).

The orphan-crop layer is advancing rapidly even where Zer0pa's narrative center stays on Tier-1 cereals. The **African Orphan Crops Consortium** (Mars/UC-Davis/ICRAF/BGI; Hendre et al. *Planta* 2019) has delivered ~6 reference genomes and ~20 in-progress assemblies including bambara groundnut, finger millet, *Lablab*, *Moringa*, and jackfruit, with the ORCAE-AOCC genome viewer hosting them under CC-BY publication terms. Cassava, chickpea (Cicer super-pangenome 2024), and pigeonpea now have multi-genome pangenome resources approaching Tier-1 quality. Pearl millet, fonio, tef, and finger millet remain at the linear-PAV-catalog stage rather than full graph pangenomes. Most African indigenous crops still lack curated reference genomes beyond AOCC publications, meaning Zer0pa would need to handle de novo assembly and annotation in many cases — but the rails are laid (Hu/Edwards *Nature Communications* 2025 on orphan crop pangenomics; the bioRxiv 2025.08 "Pangenomic Breeding Network" preprint explicitly proposes NARI-anchored pangenomes for African smallholder deployment).

The data layer for the region is thinner than it should be: ARC publishes annual cultivar evaluation reports as PDFs that need re-digitizing, SACTA publishes commercial-share tables, the SADC Seed Variety Catalogue covers harmonized cross-border trade but mainly hybrid maize and commercial vegetables, and CIMMYT's IMIS/IWIS plus Genomes-to-Fields are the public MET reference. SASSCAL WeatherNet covers climate data for Namibia/Angola/Zambia/Botswana; CHIRPS plus AgERA5 plus ARC-ISCW soil maps cover the rest. RustTracker (BGRI) provides live races and isolate locations for direct integration as priors on Sr/Lr-gene-targeted breeding. AOCC genomes through ORCAE plus IITA Genebank plus Cassavabase plus Musabase plus ICRISAT plus AfricaRice plus Genesys cover the African germplasm landscape.

The PPP funding pathways Zer0pa must encode in its scoring layer are AATF (TELA, PBR cowpea, StrigAway), HarvestPlus (Pro-Vit A maize and OFSP, iron beans, zinc rice/wheat — the Biofortification Priority Index is open data), Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation (DT maize, GOBii/Excellence in Breeding from 2023), AGRA, and SACTA itself for SA self-pollinated crops. The deployment-fit dimension of the Pareto front needs to distinguish commercial hybrid pathways (formal seed certification, SACTA, dealer networks) from smallholder OPV pathways (informal seed economy, NARI distribution, AATF-style trait-introgression), because the same predicted breeding gain has very different ROI depending on which pathway it lands in.

## Section 13. Direct answers to the ten required research questions

**1. Cleanest open-source stack for a commercially usable precision crop genomics pipeline today.** PLINK 2 (GPL-3) plus PHGv2 (Apache-2.0) plus BrAPI v2.1 plus self-hosted Breedbase (MIT) for the data spine; rrBLUP plus BGLR plus sommer (all GPL-3) plus EnvRtype (MIT) plus BGGE (GPL-3) for prediction; AlphaSimR (MIT) plus PyBrOpS (Apache-2.0) plus FielDHub (MIT) plus statgenSTA/GxE (GPL) for breeding simulation and trial design; DSSAT-CSM (BSD-3) plus AquaCrop-OSPy (GPL-3) for crop modeling — never APSIM commercially; CROPSR (Apache-2.0) plus CRISPR-PLANT v2 plus Cas-OFFinder (BSD) plus CRISPResso2 for editing; PlantCaduceus (Apache-2.0) plus HyenaDNA (BSD-3) plus PlantRNA-FM (MIT) plus AlphaFold DB (CC-BY 4.0) plus OpenFold3 (Apache-2.0) plus RFdiffusion3 (BSD) plus ProteinMPNN (MIT) for foundation models and protein design; PlantCV (MPL-2.0) plus pliman (GPL-3) plus Detectron2/SAM2/DINOv2 (Apache-2.0) plus MicMac (CECILL-B) for phenomics. AWS Open Data Registry plus Microsoft Planetary Computer Pro plus xarray/STAC for satellite compute. **Avoid**: APSIM, ASReml-R, BLUPF90, OpenDroneMap as a hosted service, Ultralytics YOLO, Google Earth Engine free tier, AgroNT and Nucleotide Transformer weights, AlphaFold3, ESM3 main weights, WorldClim 2.1, PLAZA, TAIR live data, EOX cloudless mosaics.

**2. Best open datasets for genotype–phenotype–environment modeling.** Genomes-to-Fields (G2F) maize MET as the closest "ImageNet for crop GS×E" (CC0/public-domain ag data); CIMMYT wheat panels (599-wheat, 2K wheat, Iranian wheat) as the de-facto cross-paper benchmark; 3K Rice Genomes plus IRRI phenotypes (CC-BY); 1001 Genomes Arabidopsis (CC-BY); TERRA-REF v6 sorghum and durum wheat (CC0); PhenoCam V3 (CC-BY 4.0); GWHD wheat heads (MIT); plus the AgERA5 + SoilGrids 2.0 + CHIRPS v3 + Sentinel-2 + Landsat + HLS climate-soil-RS layer. The clean composite is G2F + CIMMYT + 3KRG + AgERA5 + SoilGrids — sufficient to train and benchmark a license-clean Zer0pa GxE model end-to-end.

**3. Is pangenome-aware modeling architecture-critical or still frontier?** **Architecture-critical for the seven Tier-1 species** (maize, rice, wheat, barley, sorghum, soybean, tomato) where peer-reviewed graph pangenomes with demonstrated heritability gains exist; **partially frontier but rapidly closing** for orphan/African crops where 5–15 assemblies and PAV catalogs exist but full graph genotyping pipelines often do not; **PHGv2 is production-ready as of v2.5 (April 2026)** with TileDB-VCF backend and Apache-2.0 license, deployed across six species and BrAPI-compliant. Zer0pa's role is consumer-and-integrator, not graph-builder; build only when no public pangenome exists for a target species.

**4. Best practice for genomic selection under G×E in 2025–2026.** Tiered: Tier-1 always-on baseline is GBLUP plus Jarquín reaction-norm (`G + E + GxE` kernel sum) via BGLR/BGGE with environmental covariates from EnvRtype over critical growth windows identified by CERIS-JGRA; Tier-2 for larger-N MET is BMTME or sommer multi-trait GBLUP with factor-analytic structure (FA models open-implementation in sommer rather than ASReml); Tier-3 mechanistic for drought and heat is CGM-GS pairing DSSAT-CSM with CGM-WGP-style ABC inference on physiological parameters; Tier-4 deep learning (DNNGP, custom transformers with concatenated genotype-plus-env-covariate embeddings) only when n<500 per env or trait has known non-additive component. Validation enforces leave-one-environment-out and leave-one-year-out with reported uncertainty.

**5. Crop editing workflows practically deployable versus blocked by transformation/regeneration.** Practically deployable v1: Arabidopsis, *Nicotiana benthamiana*, tomato, rice japonica, potato, lettuce, Camelina, plus wheat-citrus-barley-triticale via UC Davis GRF-GIF licensing. Blocked or IP-encumbered: maize elites, sorghum, sugarcane (require Corteva WUS2/BBM commercial license); banana (Tropic territorial dominance); cassava (Danforth-only practical); legumes including soybean and cowpea (recalcitrance plus low transformation efficiency); cotton; sweet potato; coffee; most African indigenous crops. The under-utilized lever is VIGE — virus-induced genome editing — using BSMV in wheat/barley, FoMV in maize, TRV/PVX in tomato/*Nicotiana* — which simultaneously bypasses transformation IP gates and produces transgene-free products that clear most permissive jurisdictions as SDN-1.

**6. Best open tools for breeding simulation and cross design.** AlphaSimR (MIT) is the de facto SOTA breeding simulator; MoBPS (GPL-3) is the alternative for bitwise SNP storage and cohort-economic modeling; ChromaX (BSD-3, JAX/GPU) gives 10–100× GPU speedup for ML-coupled work; PyBrOpS (Apache-2.0) is the open multi-objective Pareto cross-design tool; AlphaMate (academic free Fortran) and optiSel (GPL-2) cover optimal contribution selection; selectiongain (GPL-2/3) gives analytical multi-stage budget optimization; FielDHub (MIT) plus DiGGer cover trial design; FieldSimR (MIT) plus statgenSTA/GxE (GPL) cover MET simulation and analysis. The proprietary MateSel and ASReml-R commercial workflows are replaceable at zero royalty cost with this open stack, although factor-analytic FA(k) MET models still require careful sommer ports.

**7. Stack strength for major cereals versus orphan crops.** Stack is strongest for maize, rice, wheat, barley, sorghum, soybean, tomato — full pangenome resources, dense genotyping panels, well-resourced breeding programs, multiple commercial editing platforms, comprehensive ontologies. Stack is genuinely emerging for cassava, banana, sweet potato, yam, chickpea, pigeonpea, common bean, cowpea — pangenome work and Breedbase instances exist; transformation remains hard. Stack is partial for pearl millet, finger millet, tef, fonio — AOCC genomes exist but graph pangenomes are early-stage. Stack is largely greenfield for bambara groundnut, marama bean, ensete, baobab, indigenous African leafy vegetables — Zer0pa would need to build de novo assembly and annotation pipelines plus extend Crop Ontology trait dictionaries. The implication is that v1 covers Tier-1 species comprehensively, v1.1 extends to AOCC priority orphan crops with reduced pangenome dependence, and v2 builds the regional African indigenous crop layer through partnership with AOCC and ARC.

**8. Regulatory bifurcations that matter most.** Three split lines dominate. **Edit type**: SDN-1 with no foreign DNA at any step is the universal minimum-friction template (clears US, AR, BR, CA, JP, AU, CN, IN, KE, NG, GH, MW, RW, England, IL, PH, TH, SG, post-2028 EU NGT-1); SDN-2 follows in most jurisdictions; SDN-3 with foreign DNA retained remains GMO almost everywhere. **Trait modifier**: herbicide tolerance triggers automatic NGT-2 in EU regardless of edit size, plus separate EPA registration in US and pesticide review in BR/AR — kills EU access for years. PIP-equivalent insect resistance via novel insecticidal protein triggers EPA PIP registration and EU NGT-1 exclusion; loss-of-function susceptibility-gene knockouts are the cleaner architectural path. **Geography**: South Africa is the single most restrictive operationally-important jurisdiction (full GMO Act for all gene editing); EU is a 2026–2028 transition jurisdiction; everywhere else is broadly permissive for SDN-1 transgene-free products.

**9. Handoff format a seed company or breeding institute would actually want.** A typed, versioned, machine-readable JSON-LD dossier with the seven blocks specified in Section 7 (Identity, Intervention Strategy, Performance Forecast, Risk, Regulatory, Provenance, Partner Handoff), regenerable from inputs at any time, with automatic CC-BY attribution emission and per-record license stamping, BrAPI v2.1 export endpoints, and recipient-specific final blocks. A SA seed company's handoff includes SACTA-compatible cultivar registration documentation; a CGIAR public institute's handoff includes ITPGRFA SMTA-compatible benefit-sharing notes; a CRISPR editing partner's handoff includes full molecular cloning specifications with WUS2/BBM, GRF-GIF, or VIGE delivery method and IP-licensing requirements explicit; a regional African agricultural program's handoff includes adoption-modeling and HarvestPlus BPI integration. The single most important schema commitment is that no implicit state, no hidden context, and no untracked manual decisions exist — the dossier is the legal substrate for partner trust.

**10. Where Zer0pa can create a proprietary compounding data flywheel analogous to PathGym.** Three substrates compound. **First, the license-stamped composite training corpus**: every dossier produced enriches Zer0pa's own license-clean training set for genomic prediction across the long tail of crops, traits, and agroecological zones not covered by G2F or CIMMYT. **Second, the regulatory-routing decision corpus**: every routing decision Zer0pa makes — which jurisdiction was selected, which trait modifier was avoided, which delivery method was chosen, what the Cartagena/Nagoya/SMTA chain looked like — becomes proprietary training data for regulatory route prediction, and no incumbent has anything comparable. **Third, the partner handoff outcome corpus**: tracking which dossiers led to actual cultivar releases, which crosses produced predicted gains, which edits regenerated successfully, which traits cleared regulatory review — closes the active-inference loop and creates a moat that compounds with every partner Zer0pa works with. The ImageNet-for-crop-GSxE benchmark (license-clean, cross-crop, with held-out test sets and standardized evaluation protocols) currently does not exist; Zer0pa should build and open-source it as a community contribution that simultaneously seeds the data flywheel.

## Section 14. Frontier opportunities not yet production-ready

Five frontier capabilities are research-grade today but worth pre-architecting Zer0pa's interfaces for. **A BoTorch-native multi-objective breeding-cycle optimizer** with AlphaSimR or ChromaX as a black-box simulator backend and qNEHVI as the acquisition function — closest production-ready code is in Diot & Iwata 2023, Azam et al. 2024, and Younis et al. 2024's released Gym environments; nothing wraps it as a service. **A causal-inference-for-trait-discovery layer** using PC-algorithm graph induction plus Mendelian-randomization effect-size pruning over multi-trait biobank-scale panels — CI-GWAS demonstrated multi-trait causal graphs from 17 traits in under 10 minutes on GPU but it is not yet packaged for production. **A plant image foundation model** trained on TERRA-REF (CC0) plus GWHD (MIT) plus PhenoBench (CC-BY-SA 4.0) plus aggregated GBIF Pl@ntNet imagery — TasselNetV4 markets itself as one but coverage is narrow. **An open ImageNet-for-crop-GSxE benchmark** spanning maize/wheat/rice/soybean/sorghum/tomato MET data with environmental covariates and a public leaderboard — unowned today and a high-leverage Zer0pa community contribution. **A reinforcement-learning-distilled breeding policy** with audit-friendly rule extraction — Younis et al. 2024 showed RL beats standard breeding practice in simulation, but no production tool distills policies into breeder-readable rules.

The frontier piece most likely to change v1 architecture during 2026 is the **plant single-cell foundation model**: scPlantLLM exists with unclear commercial licensing, and the obvious commercial path is fine-tuning scGPT (MIT) or Geneformer (Apache-2.0) on the Plant Cell Atlas. If a commercial-licensed plant scFM emerges in the next 12 months, the cell-type-resolved trait modeling it enables (especially for fruit and seed quality traits) would warrant a dedicated model branch in Zer0pa's architecture.

## Section 15. Conclusion — what changed, and what to build first

Three things in the precision crop genomics field crossed deployment thresholds in the last 18 months that materially change Zer0pa's build map. PHGv2 v2.5 made pangenome-aware genotyping production-ready for the seven Tier-1 species under Apache-2.0 licensing; this is the single most important architectural commitment Zer0pa should make. Plant DNA foundation models with permissive weights — PlantCaduceus, HyenaDNA, Evo-2, GPN, PlantRNA-FM — finally exist at sufficient quality to be used in commercial pipelines, while AgroNT and Nucleotide Transformer remain commercially unusable due to CC-BY-NC-SA weights; this means Zer0pa can adopt foundation-model G2P for sequence-level tasks (variant effect, regulatory annotation, promoter strength, splice-site detection) without license risk. The EU NGT regulation's adoption in April 2026 with mid-2028 application creates a 24-month design window where every Zer0pa-recommended edit should preemptively meet NGT-1 criteria (≤20nt, no HT, no novel insecticidal substance), giving Zer0pa partners an EU market in 2028 that competitors locked into HT or PIP traits will not have.

The build order falls out cleanly. Build the **license-stamping ETL service** and the **typed handoff dossier schema** first — these are the substrates everything else depends on, and they are also the primary moat. Build the **BrAPI-native data spine** with self-hosted Breedbase second. Wire the **Tier-1 prediction stack** (PLINK 2 + PHGv2 + rrBLUP/BGLR/sommer + EnvRtype/BGGE + DSSAT-CSM + AlphaSimR + PyBrOpS) third. Add the **regulatory router** with the master decision matrix and the Regulatory Fit Score as a Pareto axis fourth. Layer in the **plant foundation model fine-tuning pipeline** (PlantCaduceus + GPN + HyenaDNA fine-tunes for variant-effect and regulatory tasks) fifth. Add **pangenome graph genotyping** for Tier-1 species through PHGv2 plus Giraffe plus PanGenie sixth. Build the **BoTorch multi-objective optimizer** as the first frontier-to-production move seventh. Defer **plant image foundation model training**, **causal inference layer**, and **active inference reformulation** to v1.5/v2 unless a partner specifically demands them.

Zer0pa's defensible position is not the model — it is the **license-clean, regulatorily-routed, partner-friendly platform** that no incumbent currently delivers. Inari is closed; Pairwise is wet-lab-licensable but not platform-shaped; Living Models is foundation-model-only; Computomics is service-based; Cibus and Tropic are trait-licensors. The structural underfilled niche is mid-tier seed companies, CGIAR and NARES, specialty-crop programs, and the SA/SADC region — too small for Inari pricing, too sophisticated for legacy CGIAR tools, and underserved by every ML-first incumbent. The zero-IP-liability framing is genuinely valuable as long as Zer0pa stays platform-only and refuses to take trait IP — which is exactly the architectural commitment Report 1 has just specified.

Report 2 will collapse this landscape into a sharper architectural specification with concrete schema definitions, partner integration sequences, and the South African go-to-market motion through ARC-BTP, SACTA, and CIMMYT-Zimbabwe.
